The Silicon Frontier
after Moore's Law

We start from absolute zero — what "sand," "silicon," and "semiconductor" even mean — then build step by step up to wafer-scale chips, the AI compute race, and the geopolitics around it. No background needed.

Researched & built by Sashank · Sources cited throughout — open to discussion, corrections welcome

THE ONE IDEA THAT TIES IT ALL TOGETHER

For fifty years, computer chips got faster and cheaper almost automatically: every couple of years, engineers found ways to shrink the tiny switches inside them, so you could fit twice as many in the same space. That steady shrinking even had a name — Moore's Law (we'll define it properly below). But the switches are now so small they're bumping into the laws of physics, and the easy shrinking is ending. So the entire industry is scrambling to answer one question: if you can't just keep shrinking, how else do you keep making computers more powerful? This page walks from the absolute basics up to the five big answers — and the global power struggle they've triggered.

01

The words, in plain English

Let's not assume anything. Before we build a chip, here are the everyday words you'll hear constantly — each defined from scratch, in plain language. Skim the ones you know; the rest build on each other.

Here's the whole story in one sentence: we take a grain of sand, purify it into silicon, slice it into a wafer, and carve billions of tiny switches into it. Every term below is just one piece of that sentence.
Sand
…yes, really

When people say chips "start as sand," they mean it literally. Ordinary quartz sand is mostly silica — silicon dioxide (SiO₂), silicon atoms bonded to oxygen. Silicon is the second most abundant element in Earth's crust (~26%)[2], but it's never found pure; it's always locked to oxygen.

To free it, you mix high-quartz sand with carbon and heat it past 2,000°C in an electric arc furnace. The carbon grabs the oxygen and floats off as gas, leaving behind raw silicon that's about 98–99% pure.[2]

Why it matters → The raw material is essentially free and everywhere. The value isn't the sand — it's the staggering precision of everything done to it next.
Semi­conductor
the in-betweener

The word describes exactly what it does. A conductor (like copper) always lets electricity flow. An insulator (like rubber or glass) never does. A semiconductor sits in between — it conducts only when you want it to.

The reason is something called the band gap: the energy hurdle an electron must clear to move and carry current. Conductors have basically no hurdle; insulators have a huge one; semiconductors have a small one — low enough that a little heat, light, or a clever chemical tweak flips them from "off" to "on."[1]

Why it matters → That controllable on/off behavior is the whole reason computers exist. A material you can switch is a material you can compute with.
Silicon
element #14

Silicon is the specific semiconductor the industry runs on. It's a metalloid — neither fully metal nor non-metal — and it won the job because it's abundant, cheap, electrically well-behaved, and conveniently grows its own insulating "skin" (silicon dioxide) when exposed to oxygen.

For chips, that 99% silicon from the furnace isn't nearly clean enough. A further chemical purification (the Siemens process) pushes it to "eleven nines" — 99.999999999% pure. That's roughly one stray atom per billion silicon atoms.[2][2]

Quick aside: silicon (the hard, gray element) is not silicone (the soft, rubbery stuff in kitchenware and sealants). Same root word, totally different material.
Wafer
the canvas

That ultra-pure silicon is grown into a single large crystal — a cylindrical ingot (or "boule") up to 2 meters long[3] — then sliced like a deli loaf into thin, round discs. Because they're thin and round, they're called wafers.

A wafer is the surface everything gets built on. Crucially, you don't make one chip per wafer — you build hundreds at once across its face, then cut them apart at the very end. Bigger wafers (today usually 300mm across) mean more chips per slice and lower cost each.[3]

Why it matters → "Wafer-scale" — the headline technology later on — means refusing to cut the wafer up, and using the entire disc as one giant chip.
Diode
the one-way valve

The simplest useful device you can build from silicon. Take one piece doped to be n-type (extra electrons) and join it to a piece doped p-type (missing electrons) — that boundary is a PN junction, and it does something remarkable: current flows through it in one direction only.[4]

The standard analogy is a one-way valve in a water pipe — it lets flow go forward but slams shut against any backflow. Push voltage the "right" way (forward bias) and current passes; push it the "wrong" way (reverse bias) and it's blocked.

Why it matters → A transistor — the switch a chip is made of — is essentially the next step up: a PN-junction device with a third terminal that lets you turn the flow on and off. Master the diode and the transistor is a short hop. (Defined next.)
Transistor
the switch · the hero

This is the single most important component in all of electronics — the thing a chip is billions of copies of. A transistor is a tiny electrical switch with no moving parts. A diode lets current flow one way; a transistor adds a third connection — a gate — that acts like a faucet handle. Put a small voltage on the gate and current flows ("on" = 1); remove it and the flow stops ("off" = 0).

The common modern type is called a MOSFET.[5] It has three parts: the source (where current enters), the drain (where it leaves), and the gate (the controlling handle in between).

Why it matters → Everything else on this page — every "node," every "wall," every "trillion transistors" — is about making these switches smaller, faster, more numerous, and cheaper. When you read "4 trillion transistors," picture 4 trillion of these faucets on one piece of silicon.

Wait — how do switches become "computing"?

Fair question, and it's the leap most explanations skip. Here's the chain:

That's the whole magic. A computer is not fundamentally clever — it's an enormous pile of on/off switches arranged so that "flip these switches in this pattern" happens to mean "add two numbers" or "recognize a face." Intelligence emerges from staggering quantity and speed, not from any single switch being smart.

GPU
graphics processing unit

A chip you'll see mentioned constantly — and the reason Nvidia is one of the world's most valuable companies. A GPU was originally built to draw video-game graphics, which means doing the same simple math on millions of pixels at once. To do that, it crams in thousands of small calculators that all work in parallel.

It turned out that training and running AI uses the exact same kind of math — millions of small multiplications happening simultaneously. So the chips built for games became the workhorses of AI.[7] A CPU (your computer's general-purpose brain) does a few things very fast, one after another; a GPU does a huge number of things at once.

Why it matters → Nearly every technology later on is, at heart, a different bet on how to build something better than today's GPU for AI — or how to feed the GPUs we already have. The financial stakes are staggering: Nvidia now earns ~75% gross margins and posted $81.6 billion of revenue in a single quarter (early 2026, +85% year-over-year).[33]
Moore's Law
the promise that's fading

In 1965, Intel co-founder Gordon Moore noticed that the number of transistors engineers could fit on a chip was doubling about every two years.[6] That observation held for decades and became the heartbeat of the tech industry: chips reliably got twice as capable on a regular schedule, which is why your phone outclasses a 1990s supercomputer.

It was never a law of physics — just a remarkably steady trend. And it's now slowing, because transistors have shrunk to only a few atoms wide, where physics gets uncooperative and costs explode.

Related term: Dennard scaling was a companion trend — as transistors shrank, they also used proportionally less power. It broke down around 2006, which is why chips stopped getting dramatically faster and instead grew more cores.[6] These two fading trends are the reason this whole page exists.
02

Putting it together: what a chip actually is

You now know the words. Here's how three of them — silicon, doping, and the transistor — combine into a working chip, and how billions of switches get wired into something useful.

A modern AI chip is, at bottom, billions of tiny switches etched into a sliver of purified sand, connected by miles of microscopic copper wiring[8]. No single part is clever; the power comes entirely from the number of switches and how they're wired. Here's how you get from raw silicon to that.
Idea 01 · the material

Silicon, the in-betweener

A semiconductor sits between a conductor (metal) and an insulator (glass) — it conducts only when you want it to. Silicon is the workhorse: cheap, abundant in sand, and electrically well-behaved.

Chip-grade silicon must be 99.9999999% pure — "nine nines."[2][2] A single stray atom in the wrong place can ruin a circuit.

Idea 02 · the chemistry

Doping: building the switch

Pure silicon barely conducts. Doping sprinkles in trace impurities: add phosphorus (one spare electron) for n-type; add boron (one missing electron, a "hole") for p-type. Pattern these regions precisely and each becomes one transistor.

As little as 1 dopant atom per billion silicon atoms can swing conductivity by a factor of ~1,000,000.[1]

Idea 03 · the wiring

Wiring them into circuits

Individual switches are useless alone. Stacked above the transistors are up to ~15 layers of microscopic copper wiring[8] that connect them into logic gates and, ultimately, a working processor — like the floors and hallways of a city built on top of the switches.

A single advanced chip can hold tens of miles of this wiring, all in a space smaller than a fingernail.

04

The machine that prints with light

One step above deserves its own spotlight, because it's the hardest machine humanity builds — and it's the linchpin of the geopolitics later on.

EUV lithography, explained

To print features a few atoms wide, you need light with an incredibly short wavelength — 13.5 nanometers[10], "extreme ultraviolet." Making it borders on absurd: a high-power laser blasts ~50,000 droplets of molten tin per second[10], vaporizing each into a plasma that flashes out EUV light.

That light is so easily absorbed it can't travel through air or normal lenses — the whole process runs in a vacuum, bounced off ultra-smooth molybdenum/silicon mirrors (each still soaks up ~30%)[10]. The light reflects off a patterned mask and shrinks that pattern onto the resist-coated wafer.

Why this matters later → Exactly one company on Earth — ASML in the Netherlands — can build these machines.[10] That single chokepoint is what makes export controls a geopolitical weapon (Section 10).

13.5nm wavelength ~50k tin droplets/sec 1 maker: ASML

Meet EUV's older sibling: DUV

EUV is the newest, most exclusive printing light. But there's an older, cheaper one that's been around since the 2000s: DUV (deep ultraviolet), which uses a much longer 193nm wavelength of light[11]. Longer wavelength means it naturally prints chunkier features — so on its own it can't reach the smallest, most advanced chips.

The workaround is multi-patterning: instead of printing a layer in one clean shot, you expose the wafer several times with slightly shifted patterns to build up finer detail — slower, costlier, and more error-prone, but it works. This sounds like an obscure technical footnote. It's actually the crux of the entire US–China chip war, because DUV machines are cheap, widely sold, and not export-restricted — while EUV is.[11] Hold that thought for Section 10.

05

Nobody makes a chip alone

No single company designs, fabricates, and packages a frontier chip. The industry split into specialists — and understanding that split is the key to the economics and politics ahead.

✏️The "fabless" designers

Companies like Nvidia, AMD, Apple and Cerebras design chips but own no factory. They focus entirely on architecture and let someone else build it.

Why? Because a leading-edge factory is one of the most expensive objects in the private economy[12] — out of reach for all but a few.

06

Now: the walls every modern chip hits

Before the solutions make sense, you need the constraints. Three physical ceilings now govern AI hardware design — and almost every technology further down exists to climb one of them.

Wall 01

The Memory Wall

A chip's calculator and its memory (where the numbers live) are in separate places, joined by a limited number of wires. Modern AI chips can do math far faster than those wires can deliver the data — so the expensive calculator sits idle, waiting for numbers to arrive, like a chef who can cook instantly but keeps waiting on ingredients.

Engineers call this the von Neumann bottleneck (after the computer-design blueprint nearly all chips still follow). For today's AI, memory, not raw math, is the real limit.

Wall 02

The Interconnect Wall

No single chip is big enough for a large AI model, so the work is split across thousands of chips — and now moving data between them becomes the problem. Beyond a certain speed, ordinary copper wires can't carry the signal across even a server rack without it degrading and burning huge amounts of power. That ceiling pushes the industry toward sending data as light instead.

Wall 03

The Reticle Limit

The machine that prints chip patterns (Section 04) can only print one chip up to a fixed maximum size in a single shot — about 858 mm², roughly the size of a postage stamp[14]. This hard ceiling is called the reticle limit. You can't just "print a bigger chip" to get more power. Beating this ceiling is the entire premise of wafer-scale engineering.

07

Five answers to the same problem

Each approach below attacks the walls differently. Tap a tab to open a full walk-through — what problem it solves, how it actually works, an everyday analogy, and the catch. Wafer-scale and packaging go deepest.

Wafer-Scale Integration

Climbs · the reticle limit + the interconnect wall

The bet: instead of cutting a wafer into hundreds of separate chips and wiring them back together, keep the entire wafer as one giant processor. Cerebras's WSE-3 does exactly this — a single chip 21.5 cm on a side, the size of a dinner plate.[15]

Why it wins: remember the interconnect wall — data crawling between separate chips? On one continuous wafer, that data never leaves home. It moves on-chip at 21 petabytes per second[15], thousands of times faster than chips talking over cables. For AI, where the bottleneck is feeding data fast enough, that's transformative.

Why does AI care so much about this? When a chatbot generates text, it must read through its entire multi-billion-number "brain" to produce each single word, then do it again for the next word. That's less about raw math and more about shoveling enormous amounts of data fast — exactly what keeping everything on one giant chip enables.

How do you beat the reticle limit? — field stitching

The printing machine still can't expose anything bigger than ~858 mm². So how is the chip 50× larger?[14] The trick is field stitching. The foundry prints 84 identical tiles across the wafer[14] — exactly as if making 84 normal chips — but then, instead of sawing them apart, it adds extra steps to print short wires across the gaps (the "scribe lines" that normally get cut through).

Over one million wires bridge those seams[14], fusing 84 tiles into one continuous circuit. To the software, the seams simply don't exist — it sees a single enormous chip.

How does a dinner-plate chip survive defects? — redundancy

Every wafer has microscopic flaws — normally you just throw away the few bad chips. But you can't throw away part of a single giant chip. Cerebras's answer: build the chip from tiny cores (~900,000 of them) and quietly include ~1.5% spares[15]. When a flaw kills a core, on-chip logic routes around it and swaps in a neighbor — the chip doesn't even notice.

This flips intuition on its head: because each core is so small, a defect wastes a speck of silicon instead of a whole chip. The giant chip ends up as reliable to manufacture as a normal one.

Analogy → A normal chip is a single sheet of glass — one crack and it's scrap. A wafer-scale chip is a mosaic of tiny tiles: a few cracked tiles barely matter, and you keep spares in the box to swap in.

⚡ Where it shines

Running today's largest AI models fast (inference). Deployed at Meta, Mayo Clinic, the US Dept. of Defense[16]; reported to answer many times faster than GPU systems.

⚠ The catch

Feeding a 23-kilowatt chip power and cooling is brutal (300+ voltage regulators, custom liquid cooling)[15]. And it's locked to TSMC — a decade of co-engineering a rival can't quickly copy. Its May 2026 IPO priced at a ~$66B valuation on just $510M of revenue — richly valued, with 86% of sales from two customers.[41][40]

Advanced Packaging — the unsung hero

Climbs · the memory wall · "if you can't shrink it, stack it"

If you can't make a single transistor much smaller, change strategy: take several separate pieces of silicon — called chiplets — and pack them together so tightly they behave like one chip. This is advanced packaging, and it's quietly become the most important battleground in chipmaking.

The headline product is putting an AI processor right next to its memory on a shared base, with a huge, ultra-short connection between them — directly attacking the memory wall.

The three building blocks (in plain terms)

The interposer — a thin slab of silicon that acts like a super-dense "circuit board within the chip." Chiplets sit on top of it, connected by wiring far finer than any normal board allows.

TSVs (through-silicon vias) — microscopic vertical tunnels drilled through silicon so signals can pass straight up and down between stacked layers, instead of routing around the edges.

HBM (high-bandwidth memory) — memory chips stacked like a tower[18] (using those TSVs) instead of laid flat, putting far more memory in reach of the processor. This stacked memory is the single hottest commodity in AI hardware.

📐 2.5D — side by side

Chiplets placed next to each other on the interposer. The proven, dominant approach today (e.g. TSMC's CoWoS). Easier to cool since the hot parts sit apart.

🏢 3D — stacked tall

Chiplets stacked directly on top of each other and fused with hybrid bonding (a copper-to-copper weld with no bumps[17]). Shortest possible connections — but trapping heat between layers is hard.

Analogy → 2.5D is a bungalow — rooms spread out, easy to cool, but a big footprint. 3D is a skyscraper — minimal land, everything close, but the upper floors cook the lower ones. The industry uses both depending on the job.

Why this is THE bottleneck → The processors exist; the world just can't package them fast enough. This packaging capacity (CoWoS) and the stacked memory (HBM) have been sold out into 2026[19] — which is why value is shifting from "who prints the smallest transistor" to "who can assemble and feed it." In hard dollars: HBM memory alone is ~45% of the cost of a flagship AI GPU, versus ~14% for the logic chip itself.[37]

Silicon Photonics & Co-Packaged Optics

Climbs · the interconnect wall · move data with light, not electrons

Recall the interconnect wall: copper can't carry data fast over distance without huge power loss. The fix is to stop using electricity for the journey and use light instead — the same idea as the fiber-optic internet, but shrunk down and moved onto the chip's package.

Today, that light-conversion happens in bulky plug-in modules at the edge of the board. Co-packaged optics moves it right next to the processor, so signals turn into light immediately. Nvidia's Quantum-X and Spectrum-X switches (built on TSMC's COUPE platform)[20] do this.

How does light carry data?

A tiny laser flickers on and off incredibly fast — each flash and gap is a 1 or a 0, the same bits as before, just carried by photons instead of electrons. Light through a glass fiber barely weakens over distance and generates far less heat than pushing electricity through copper, so you can move vastly more data for less power.

Analogy → Copper wiring is like shouting across a noisy room — fine up close, exhausting and garbled across a stadium. Optical is like a laser pointer — it stays crisp and tight no matter the distance.

⚡ Where it shines

Wiring together the "AI factories" of a million GPUs, where data must travel rack-to-rack. The enabling layer for the largest AI systems being built now.

⚠ The catch

Aligning lasers and fibers to within a fraction of a hair's width is fiendishly hard, and only a few TSMC/Intel facilities can do it — concentrating yet another chokepoint.

Neuromorphic & Analog In-Memory

Climbs · the memory wall · rethink the whole design

The most radical approach: stop separating memory and compute at all. Today's chips shuttle data back and forth between them (the memory wall). The brain doesn't — it stores and processes information in the same place, which is part of why it runs on about 20 watts[21] while an AI supercomputer burns millions.

Neuromorphic chips (Intel's Loihi, IBM's NorthPole) copy that idea: many tiny units that each hold a little memory and do a little computing, firing only when there's something to do — rather than marching to a constant clock.

The key idea: "fire only when needed"

A normal chip computes on every tick of its clock, whether or not anything changed — like a factory line running full speed even with nothing on the belt. Neuromorphic chips work in spikes: a unit stays silent until it receives a meaningful signal, then briefly fires. Most of the chip is idle most of the time, which is why it sips power.

Analogy → A standard processor is a sprinter running in place nonstop. A neuromorphic chip is a cat — still and quiet until something moves, then a sudden burst, then still again.

⚡ Where it shines

Battery-powered "edge" devices — robots, drones, always-on sensors — where every watt counts. A Loihi-based robot reportedly ran for days on a charge.

⚠ The honest catch

The hardware is ahead of the software. The programming tools are immature and these "spiking" networks are hard to train, so this shines in niches — not yet a GPU replacement.

The 2nm Node & Gate-All-Around

The classical lever — not dead, just harder and pricier

The old playbook — just shrink the transistor — isn't dead, only slowing. And making it work now requires reinventing the transistor's shape. A "node" (like "2nm" or "3nm") is roughly a generation of manufacturing; smaller numbers mean denser, more efficient chips.

TSMC's 2nm generation reached mass production in late 2025[22], introducing a new transistor design called Gate-All-Around — and a follow-on, A16 (~late 2026), adds a clever power-delivery trick.

The two innovations, in plain terms

🔲 Gate-All-Around (GAA)

The "gate" is the part that switches a transistor on and off. As transistors shrank, the gate lost control and current started leaking. GAA wraps the gate around the channel on all four sides instead of three — gripping it tightly again so it switches cleanly and wastes less power.

🔌 Backside power delivery

Normally, power and data wires compete for space on top of the chip. This moves the power wiring underneath, freeing the top purely for data — like moving a building's plumbing to the basement so the floors aren't cluttered with pipes.

Why this belongs on the list → Each new node now delivers smaller gains at sharply higher cost. The shrinking payoff from shrinking is exactly why the other four approaches above exist — the industry can no longer rely on this lever alone.

⚖️The mechanism worth understanding: why memory ate everything

Here's the second-order insight most coverage misses. Building HBM memory consumes roughly 3× the wafer capacity per bit of ordinary memory (DDR5), because the chips are larger and stacking lowers yields. So every wafer redirected to feed AI GPUs is ~3 wafers pulled out of the ordinary memory supply.[43]

The result is a chain reaction an analyst can trace end to end: HBM is sold out through 2026[43] → ordinary memory (DRAM) gets crowded out → DRAM contract prices jumped ~90% in a single quarter in early 2026[43] → the cost shows up in everyday products, with Nvidia even cutting some gaming-GPU production 30–40% as memory is diverted to data centers.[43] New fabs won't add meaningful supply until 2027 or later, so the squeeze is structural, not a quick blip.[43]

The takeaway → This is why the memory makers (SK hynix, Samsung, Micron — ~95% of supply) suddenly print record margins, and why "the price of RAM in your laptop" is now downstream of "how many AI data centers got built." One bottleneck, rippling outward.

In May 2026, wafer-scale pioneer Cerebras went public — the cleanest real-money test yet of whether a credible Nvidia challenger can command a premium. It's a perfect analyst case study, because the bull case and the red flags sit right next to each other.

  • Profit mirage: the $238M "net income" was a one-time $363M accounting gain — the real operating result was a $146M loss.[40]
  • Thin margins: just 39% gross margin in 2025 — versus Nvidia's 75% — so even at scale the economics are far less rich than the leader's.[41]
  • Single supplier: 100% dependent on TSMC, with no guaranteed allocation. And at the first-day close it traded at ~130× sales, pricing in near-flawless execution.[41]

Snapshot caveat: Cerebras reports its first results as a public company on June 23, 2026 — so these figures are a debut-moment picture that the market is about to re-price on real numbers.[41]

The question every analyst is really asking: is this a bubble?

Stack the facts and the tension is obvious. On one side: ~$700B of annual spending, ~80% gross margins, sold-out supply chains, and a memory "supercycle" some compare to the 1990s boom. On the other: AI revenue that hasn't yet caught up to the depreciation that all that hardware creates, GPUs that are short-lived assets (roughly two-thirds of Microsoft's capex went to fast-depreciating chips), and valuations pricing in flawless execution.[38]

The honest answer → Nobody knows yet. The bull says compute is the new oil and whoever's short on it loses the decade. The bear says synchronized $700B bets by four firms create correlated risk, and that memory prices — like every cycle before — eventually correct. Both can cite hard numbers. That's exactly what makes it worth watching rather than declaring.

What an analyst actually watches from here

Rather than calling a top, these are the specific signals that would confirm or break the thesis — the dials a buy-side analyst keeps on the desk:

  • AI revenue vs. depreciation: do cloud AI run-rates start covering the cost of the hardware being written down? The single most important number.[38]
  • Hyperscaler discipline: any guide-down in capex (Meta's stock already fell ~6–9% on a raise) would signal the demand side blinking.[38]
  • Memory ASPs: the first quarter HBM/DRAM prices stop rising marks the cycle turning — historically the most reliable tell.[43]
  • Custom silicon: how fast Google's TPUs and Amazon's Trainium take share from Nvidia — the threat to that 80% / 75%-margin position.[33]
  • Power, not chips: the binding constraint is shifting from GPU supply to electricity and grid interconnects — watch the energy deals.[38]

The layer no spec sheet shows: politics

Computing power has become national power — so chips are now a tool of statecraft, like oil or weapons. But the recent drama only makes sense with the full backstory. Below, two intertwined threads over the last decade: the AI technology boom and the US–China chip war — which grew up together.

Why are chips a weapon? Whoever leads in AI may lead in economic and military power — and AI runs on the most advanced chips. The US and its allies happen to control the critical chokepoints: chip designs (Nvidia), the manufacturing (TSMC in Taiwan), and above all the EUV machines (ASML, the Netherlands) needed to make the very best chips. So the US has tried to restrict China's access to those chips and tools, hoping to slow its AI progress. China's response — and whether the strategy is even working — is the genuinely surprising part.
AI & chip technology Policy & geopolitics
2012–2017 · The spark
AI quietly comes alive on gaming chips — years before most people noticed.
2012

AlexNet: AI wakes up on GPUs

A research team uses two Nvidia gaming GPUs to win an image-recognition contest by a stunning margin[7] — proving that graphics chips could power AI. This is the spark that eventually made Nvidia one of the world's most valuable companies.

2016

The wafer-scale bet begins

Nvidia hands the first DGX-1 AI supercomputer to a young lab called OpenAI.[7][16] The same year, a startup named Cerebras is founded on a contrarian idea: build one chip the size of a whole wafer (the technology from Section 07).

2017

The "Transformer" is invented

Google researchers publish a new AI design called the Transformer.[23] It's the architecture behind ChatGPT and essentially every modern AI — and it's enormously hungry for the kind of parallel math GPUs excel at. The demand engine is now built.

2018–2020 · The chip war opens
The US starts treating chips as national-security leverage against China.
2018

The first shot: ZTE

The US briefly bans American companies from selling to ZTE, a Chinese telecom firm[24] — nearly killing it overnight because it depended on US chips. The episode is a wake-up call in Beijing: relying on foreign chips is a strategic vulnerability.

2019

The big one: Huawei blacklisted

The US adds Huawei — China's tech champion — to the "Entity List,"[24] cutting it off from US technology. China responds by making chip self-sufficiency a top national-security priority, pouring in over $100 billion.[24]

2020

China's top factory is restricted

The US restricts SMIC, China's most advanced chip factory[25], blocking its access to the best Western tools — including, critically, the EUV machines needed for cutting-edge chips. China is now boxed out of the frontier… on paper.

2022 · Everything goes vertical
AI explodes into public view — and the policy walls go up the same year.
Aug 2022

The US tries to bring chips home: the CHIPS Act

Worried that ~90% of the most advanced chips are made in Taiwan[26], the US commits $52.7 billion to rebuild domestic chipmaking.[26] (Three decades ago the US made ~37% of the world's chips; by 2022, ~12%.)[26]

Oct 7, 2022

The landmark blockade

The US issues its broadest-ever chip rule: no advanced AI chips, and no equipment to make them, for China.[27] This is the moment export controls became a true technology blockade — the foundation everything since builds on.

Nov 2022

ChatGPT lands — demand goes nuclear

OpenAI releases ChatGPT; it hits 100 million users in two months.[23] Suddenly every company on Earth wants AI, every AI needs GPUs, and the most advanced chips become the most fought-over hardware on the planet. The stakes of the chip war just multiplied.

2023–2026 · Does the blockade work?
China finds ways around the wall — and the policy starts to wobble.
2023

The Mate 60 shock

Huawei releases a phone with an advanced 7nm chip — made domestically by SMIC[28], despite the blockade, using DUV multi-patterning. Washington is stunned: the controls were supposed to make this impossible. The "are they even working?" debate begins in earnest.

Late 2025

TSMC starts "2nm" production

The most advanced chip generation yet enters mass production — using the new Gate-All-Around transistor design from earlier[22] — with early quality (yield) rates rivals haven't been able to match. The technical lead stays with Taiwan.

Dec 2025

The surprise reversal on the H200

The H200 is one of Nvidia's most powerful AI chips. After banning its sale to China, the US administration abruptly signals it will allow it — reportedly in exchange for a ~25% cut of the revenue going to the US government.[29] An unusual mix of trade policy and national security.

Jan 13–15, 2026

The rule is made official — and called incoherent

A formal rule lets Nvidia's H200 and AMD's competing chip go to China only with case-by-case approval, a 25% tariff, and quantity limits.[29] Critics on both sides hated it: too loose for security hawks, too restrictive for industry. Pleasing no one.

Jan 2026

The twist: China says "no thanks"

In a remarkable reversal, reports say Chinese authorities told companies not to buy the very chips the US had just approved[30] — pushing them to use domestic chips instead, to build self-reliance. The buyer refused the sale.

Q1 2026

The "loophole": China routes around the ban

Remember DUV — the older, unrestricted printing light? Analysis shows China's foundry (SMIC) and Huawei using DUV with multi-patterning (printing a layer in several passes) to make near-cutting-edge AI chips without the banned EUV machines. It's slower and more wasteful — only ~20% of chips come out usable — but it works, and it's scaling.[31]

Apr 2026

Cerebras files to go public

The wafer-scale pioneer files paperwork for a stock-market debut (an "S-1" is the document a company files before an IPO), targeting a ~$22–25B value on ~$510M of 2025 revenue.[16] A real-money test of whether the giant-chip bet works as a business.

The deeper, uncomfortable question → Step back and look at the full decade: each escalation since 2018 was meant to slow China down — yet it may have forced China to build its own industry, pouring money into domestic factories and equipment makers (one, SiCarrier, is openly trying to build a "Chinese ASML"[32]) that might never have existed otherwise. Huawei was nearly dead in 2019; by 2023 it shipped an advanced chip anyway. So did the controls slow China down, or speed up its independence? Genuinely contested — and a far better question to debate than to answer with confidence.

Worth arguing about — I don't think these are settled

On wafer-scaleIs it a durable architecture, or a brilliant niche for bandwidth-bound inference that GPUs eventually absorb? Cerebras's IPO will test the thesis in public.
On the bottleneckIf packaging and memory — not transistors — are the real constraint, should we even still call this a "Moore's Law" story?
On the money~$700B in annual capex against AI revenue that hasn't caught up to the depreciation. Disciplined long-term investment, or the late innings of a bubble? Both sides cite hard numbers.
